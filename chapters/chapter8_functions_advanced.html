Chapter 8: Advanced Functions & Logarithms

Introduction

Advanced functions focus on the relationship between exponential growth and logarithmic scales. In this chapter, we explore how to find the Inverse of an exponential function and how the Logarithmic Law allows us to solve for variables in the exponent.

Core Rules & Formulas

  • Exponential Form: \( y = b^x \) (where \( b > 0, b \neq 1 \))
  • Logarithmic Form (The Inverse): \( x = \log_b y \)
  • Log Laws for Equations:
    • \( \log_b(A \times C) = \log_b A + \log_b C \)
    • \( \log_b(A^n) = n \log_b A \)
  • Restriction: The argument of a log must be positive (\( y > 0 \)).

Worked Examples

Example 1: Finding the Inverse
Given \( f(x) = 3^x \). Find \( f^{-1}(x) \).
1. Swap \( x \) and \( y \): \( x = 3^y \)
2. Convert to log form: \( y = \log_3 x \)
3. Answer: \( f^{-1}(x) = \log_3 x \)
Example 2: Solving for \( n \) (Finance Link)
Solve for \( n \): \( 2 = (1.05)^n \)
1. Take logs of both sides: \( \log 2 = \log (1.05)^n \)
2. Use power law: \( \log 2 = n \log 1.05 \)
3. \( n = \frac{\log 2}{\log 1.05} \approx 14.21 \)

Chapter Exercises

  1. Find the inverse of \( f(x) = (\frac{1}{2})^x \). Sketch both on the same set of axes.
  2. Solve for \( x \) without a calculator: \( \log_2 x + \log_2(x-2) = 3 \).
  3. The graph of \( f(x) = \log_a x \) passes through the point \( (9; 2) \). Calculate the value of \( a \).

Summary & Conclusions

  • The y-intercept of \( y = b^x \) becomes the x-intercept of \( y = \log_b x \).
  • Exponential functions have a horizontal asymptote; Logarithmic functions have a vertical asymptote.
  • Logarithms are the primary tool for solving financial problems involving time (\( n \)).
