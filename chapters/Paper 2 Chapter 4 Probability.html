Introduction

Probability measures the **likelihood of events** occurring. In Paper 2, learners focus on **both theoretical and experimental probability**, including **combined events, independent and dependent events**.

Basic Probability Rules

  • 0 ≤ P(A) ≤ 1
  • P(S) = 1, where S is the sample space
  • P(A') = 1 − P(A), probability of complement
  • For mutually exclusive events: P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B)

Combined Events

Probability of two events occurring together (intersection):

  • Independent events: P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B)
  • Dependent events: P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B|A)

Worked Example 1 – Independent Events

Two dice are rolled. Find probability of both showing 6.

  1. P(6 on die 1) = 1/6, P(6 on die 2) = 1/6
  2. P(both) = 1/6 × 1/6 = 1/36

Worked Example 2 – Dependent Events

Two cards drawn without replacement. Find probability both are aces.

  1. P(first ace) = 4/52
  2. P(second ace) = 3/51
  3. P(both) = 4/52 × 3/51 = 12/2652 = 1/221

Complementary Probability

Probability that an event does not occur: P(A') = 1 − P(A)

Worked Example 3

A bag has 5 red and 3 blue balls. Find probability of not drawing a red ball.

  1. Total balls = 5 + 3 = 8
  2. P(red) = 5/8
  3. P(not red) = 1 − 5/8 = 3/8

Conditional Probability

P(A|B) = Probability of A given B has occurred

Worked Example 4

In a deck of 52 cards, find probability that a card is a king given it is a face card.

  1. Total face cards = 12
  2. Number of kings = 4
  3. P(King | Face card) = 4/12 = 1/3

Practice Exercises

  1. A coin is tossed twice. Find probability of two heads.
  2. Find probability of drawing a red card from a standard deck.
  3. Two balls drawn with replacement: probability both are white.
  4. Find probability of not rolling a 4 on a die.
  5. Conditional probability: probability that a student is female given that they are in Grade 12 (use hypothetical numbers).

Summary

  • Probability ranges from 0 to 1
  • Complement: P(A') = 1 − P(A)
  • Independent vs dependent events differ in calculation
  • Conditional probability helps in dependent scenarios
  • Step-by-step reasoning avoids errors
