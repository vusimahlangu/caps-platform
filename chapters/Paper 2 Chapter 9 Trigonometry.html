Chapter 9: Trigonometry (2D & 3D)

Introduction

In Paper 2, Trigonometry extends into the real world. We use the Sine, Cosine, and Area rules to solve for distances and heights in two and three dimensions, often involving shadows, towers, or ships at sea.

Core Rules & Formulas

  • The Sine Rule: \(\frac{a}{\sin A} = \frac{b}{\sin B} = \frac{c}{\sin C}\) (Used for AAS or SSA).
  • The Cosine Rule: \(a^2 = b^2 + c^2 - 2bc \cos A\) (Used for SAS or SSS).
  • The Area Rule: \(Area = \frac{1}{2}ab \sin C\) (Used when two sides and an included angle are known).
  • Angle of Elevation: The angle measured upwards from the horizontal.

Worked Examples

Example 1: Using the Sine Rule
In \(\triangle ABC\), \(\hat{A} = 40^\circ\), \(\hat{B} = 60^\circ\), and side \(b = 12\)cm. Find side \(a\).
Step: \(\frac{a}{\sin 40^\circ} = \frac{12}{\sin 60^\circ}\)
Result: \(a = \frac{12 \sin 40^\circ}{\sin 60^\circ} \approx 8.91\)cm.
Example 2: The Cosine Rule (3D Context)
Two points on the ground are 50m apart. A tower top has angles of elevation from these points. Use the Cosine Rule to find the distance between point A and the base of the tower.

Chapter Exercises

  1. In \(\triangle PQR\), \(p = 8\), \(q = 10\), and \(\hat{R} = 120^\circ\). Calculate the length of \(r\).
  2. A flagstaff is \(h\) meters tall. From a point \(A\), the angle of elevation is \(\alpha\). Prove that the distance from \(A\) to the base is \(h \cot \alpha\).
  3. Find the area of \(\triangle XYZ\) if \(x = 7\), \(y = 9\), and \(\hat{Z} = 45^\circ\).

Summary & Conclusions

  • Use the Sine Rule when you have a matching "Angle and Opposite Side" pair.
  • Use the Cosine Rule when you are "boxed in" by two sides and an included angle.
  • In 3D problems, always identify the common side between the horizontal and vertical triangles.
