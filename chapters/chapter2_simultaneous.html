Chapter 2: Simultaneous Equations

Solving for two variables ($x$ and $y$) simultaneously.

1. Introduction

Simultaneous equations find the point of intersection between two graphs. In Grade 12, we typically solve one linear equation and one quadratic equation.

2. Rules: The Substitution Method

  1. Make one variable the subject of the linear equation (e.g., $y = \dots$).
  2. Substitute this expression into the quadratic equation.
  3. Solve the resulting quadratic equation for the first variable.
  4. Substitute your answers back to find the second variable.

3. Examples

Solve for $x$ and $y$ simultaneously:

\[ y + 2x = 2 \quad \text{and} \quad x^2 - y = 1 \]

Step 1: $y = 2 - 2x$

Step 2: $x^2 - (2 - 2x) = 1 \implies x^2 + 2x - 3 = 0$

Step 3: $(x + 3)(x - 1) = 0 \implies x = -3 \text{ or } x = 1$

4. Illustration: The Intersection

Memory Anchor: The points where the line and curve cross are the solutions. Drag the line to see the solutions change.

5. Chapter Exercise

Solve for $x$ and $y$:

  1. $x - y = 3$ and $x^2 + xy - y^2 = 7$
  2. $2x + y = 1$ and $x^2 + y^2 = 25$

6. Summary & Real-Life Principles

Finding: Simultaneous equations represent a "meeting point."

Perception: In economics, this is used to find the Equilibrium Point where Supply meets Demand.
