Chapter 5: Statistics & Regression

Introduction

Paper 2 Statistics focuses on interpreting data relationships and distributions. We move from single-variable data (Mean, Standard Deviation) to Bivariate Data, where we analyze how one variable affects another using scatter plots and lines of best fit.

Core Rules & Formulas

  • Standard Deviation (\(\sigma\)): Measures data dispersion. A higher \(\sigma\) indicates more spread-out data.
  • The Five-Number Summary: Min, \(Q_1\), Median (\(Q_2\)), \(Q_3\), and Max.
  • Regression Line: \(\hat{y} = a + bx\), where \(b\) is the gradient and \(a\) is the y-intercept.
  • Correlation Coefficient (\(r\)): Describes the strength and direction (\(-1 \leq r \leq 1\)).

Worked Examples

Example 1: The Ogive (Cumulative Frequency)
Plot the cumulative frequency at the upper class boundaries. The curve always starts at the lower boundary of the first class with a frequency of zero.
Example 2: Interpreting Skewness
If Mean > Median, the data is Positively Skewed (Skewed to the right). If Mean < Median, it is Negatively Skewed.
Example 3: Outlier Detection
An outlier is any value outside: \([Q_1 - 1.5 \times IQR]\) or \([Q_3 + 1.5 \times IQR]\).

Illustration: Scatter Plot & Regression

Drag the data points to see how the Line of Best Fit adjusts. Notice how moving a single point far away (an outlier) pulls the entire line towards it.

Chapter Exercises

  1. Draw a box-and-whisker plot for: 12, 15, 17, 22, 25, 28, 30.
  2. Use your calculator to find the correlation coefficient \(r\) for the data: (1;10), (2;18), (3;25), (4;33).
  3. Estimate the median from an ogive where the total frequency \(n = 80\).

Summary & Conclusions

  • Ogives are used to find medians and percentiles.
  • Regression lines predict trends; \(r\) tells you if that prediction is reliable.
  • Always identify Outliers as they distort the mean and standard deviation.
