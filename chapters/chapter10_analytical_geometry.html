Chapter 10: Vectors

Introduction

Vectors represent quantities that have both magnitude and direction. In Paper 2, we use vectors to describe points in space and find the inclination between two lines using algebraic components rather than traditional geometric proofs.

Core Rules & Formulas

  • Component Form: \(\vec{v} = x\mathbf{i} + y\mathbf{j}\) or \(\vec{v} = (x; y)\).
  • Magnitude: \(|\vec{v}| = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}\).
  • Scalar (Dot) Product: \(\vec{u} \cdot \vec{v} = x_1x_2 + y_1y_2\).
  • The Angle Theorem: \(\vec{u} \cdot \vec{v} = |\vec{u}| |\vec{v}| \cos \theta\).
  • Orthogonality: If \(\vec{u} \cdot \vec{v} = 0\), the vectors are perpendicular.

Worked Examples

Example 1: Magnitude Calculation
Given \(\vec{u} = (5; -12)\). Find the magnitude.
Step: \(|\vec{u}| = \sqrt{5^2 + (-12)^2} = \sqrt{25 + 144} = 13\).
Example 2: Finding the Angle
Calculate the angle between \(\vec{a} = (3; 4)\) and \(\vec{b} = (4; 3)\).
1. \(\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = (3)(4) + (4)(3) = 24\).
2. \(|\vec{a}| = 5\) and \(|\vec{b}| = 5\).
3. \(\cos \theta = \frac{24}{5 \times 5} = 0.96 \Rightarrow \theta \approx 16.26^\circ\).

Chapter Exercises

  1. Find the value of \(k\) if \(\vec{a} = (k; 3)\) and \(\vec{b} = (4; -2)\) are perpendicular.
  2. Given \(\vec{u} = (-3; 4)\), find the unit vector in the direction of \(\vec{u}\).
  3. Calculate the angle between \(\vec{p} = (2; 1)\) and \(\vec{q} = (1; 2)\).

Summary & Conclusions

  • Magnitude is essentially the Pythagorean theorem applied to coordinate pairs.
  • The Dot Product allows us to solve for angles in Paper 2 without complex geometry.
  • A dot product of zero is the most common test for perpendicularity in exams.
